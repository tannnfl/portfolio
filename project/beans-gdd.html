Document Overview

Design document for Beans and Guns — an online 3D collaborative puzzle sandbox. Covers the full mechanic design for the asynchronous relay system, fog reveal logic, item–scene relationship mapping, and the Unity Editor tooling built to support content authoring.

Contents

  • Async relay mechanic: 10 items per player per session, 50-item scene cap; FIFO eviction removes oldest items when cap is exceeded; concurrent submissions resolved sequentially
  • Item system: 3D models with hidden numerical attributes; shared unlimited pool displayed as 5-slot backpack refreshed randomly; items float slowly and collide
  • Three-phase structure: Free Creation (0–50% similarity, ending locks at 50%) → Locked Iteration (50–90%, guide toward locked ending) → Forgetting (click items to dissolve, max 5/turn, win when all gone)
  • Six childhood-themed endings: bus station, school grounds, grandfather's house, bedroom, abstract space, nostalgic computer desktop; similarity scoring algorithm matches room property totals against each ending's target profile
  • Visual progression: post-processing shader suite dynamically shifts hue, contrast, saturation, dither pattern, outline weight, and blur based on room property totals
  • Audio design (FMOD): dynamic music scales with item count; spatial audio fades in on world window hover; quiets during forgetting phase
  • Spirit NPCs: each scene contains a bound spirit that communicates with players; freeing all spirits across six scenes is the ultimate goal
  • World view & playback: six ending thumbnails; hover rewinds scene changes chronologically, click pauses; each submission captured as screenshot for replay
  • Unity Editor tooling: hint text authoring window, item–scene relationship mapping editor

Design Process

The FIFO eviction rule — oldest items removed when the scene cap is hit — emerged as the most generative constraint in the design. Players can't just accumulate; they must build with intention. Documenting this helped the team understand why the rule was load-bearing and shouldn't be relaxed even when playtesters initially found it frustrating.
